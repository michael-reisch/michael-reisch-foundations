Problem Solving

Feeling Blocked:

One problem I struggled with was the stretch challenge on the gradebook kata. The problem asked that you basically take each individual step that you did in the previous steps of the kata, and combine them into one function that takes in the students, and the scores and then adds them to the gradebook. I really wanted to be able add the students and then their respective scores to them using one of the methods we had been learning, but ultimately had to use dot notation to add each student one at a time, and then assign them to the appropriate index of the scores array like this:

gradebook.Joseph.testsScores = scores [0]

This worked, but it wasn’t dynamic, so that if the array of students or scores ever changed, the function wouldn’t…. function. So, I solved the challenge, but am definitely aware that there’s better ways I could be doing things. So much room for growth!

I used every problem solving technique at my disposal to solve the problem in the way I wanted to: pseudocode, googling, trial and error, error messages, console.logging, etc. But ultimately came up empty handed and settled for the problem being solved, even if it wasn’t in the way I wanted it to be solved.

Elegance

One problem that I solved elegantly was the deBee function in the Built-in Methods replit challenge. Through a bit of googling I discovered the replace() method and how you can use regular expressions (RegEx) to specify string patterns to be replaced and then what you want those specific patterns to be replaced with. The ‘hints.js’ file suggested using split, then filter then join, etc. I didn’t look at ‘hints.js’ until after I completed the problem so I was proud that I was able to find an alternative and less verbose method.

Through this I learned the power of googling. Most problems we will encounter will have been solved before by people who have been doing programming much longer than us. And, thankfully, many of their solutions are freely available on the internet. May as well seek them out!

How Do I Feel About:

Pseudocode

I don’t feel super confident with pseudocode yet. For most of these problems, I’m able to hold the function construction in my mind as I try to solve it. Admittedly, I just jump right in to trying to solve the problem before doing pseudocode and only turn to pseudocode when I’m stuck. Then, after jotting down the basic structure of what I’m trying to do, an idea will pop into my head, which I then test out. Sometimes it works, sometimes it doesn’t. But the pseudocode is definitely an effective way of taking a more generalized perspective of the problem.

Trying Something

I think of this technique as ‘trial and error’. Just kind of messing around, moving pieces around, seeing what the outcome is to get a deeper understanding of what’s going on. I quite like this technique because it’s quite playful. Like a kid playing with blocks, seeing how many ways they can assemble them, how the blocks balance, does it fall over? How high can I go? Oh.. if I build a stronger foundation, it’s stronger overall. It’s fun!

Rubber Ducky Method

This is one that I haven’t really tried yet, as all the other methods have served me well. Although sometimes, when I’m stuck, I do talk out loud to myself. Just kind of saying out loud what I’m trying to do. Certainly doesn’t hurt!

Console.logging

This is a great method, because you can return different variables and such to see if what you think is happening, is actually happening. You can move it around, see what’s returning at different parts of your program. I feel quite confident with this technique. I think of this as being a tool in the trial and error process.

Googling

Good ol’ Google. My friend, my oracle. I probably spend more time Googling things than I do writing actual code. I’m hoping this ratio will reverse as I get more skilled in programming, but for now, I really see it as a method of learning, not just problem solving. By researching, seeking things out, you’re broadening your exposure to so many different methods and techniques. What you’re reading on Google might not solve your current problem, but it might help with a future one!

Asking Peers/Coaches for Help

As I expected, being my stubborn self, I have not reached out for help yet. I’ve been able to solve every problem I’ve encountered so far (under time) through the aforementioned techniques. I’m sure this track record won’t last forever. There’s a problem waiting for me in the future where all these techniques will be exercises in futility. I know that when this time comes, I’ll reach out for help.

Reflection

As much as I want to focus solely on the technical aspects of programming, reflection is definitely important. It cements the concepts I’m learning well into my mind. They settle like sand in a jar full of marbles; filling in all the nooks and crannies. Lately, I’ve been having dreams about programming. Also, I’m feeling my mind change. The way thoughts form feels more efficient. Like they jump from thought to thought faster and in a more creative way.
